# Memory: Chris Allen

## Personal Profile

Chris Allen is a third-generation Irish-American who grew up in Kailua on O'ahu's windward side. His maternal grandparents immigrated from County Cork; his paternal side traces back to Boston. The cultural inheritance shows up in specific places: the hospitality instinct that feels less learned than inherited, the weight he places on loyalty and keeping one's word, the stubborn streak his mother Dorothy would recognize immediately. Growing up in Hawai'i layered something else onto that foundation: respect for the ocean as teacher rather than backdrop, the aloha spirit that is easier to feel than define.

He holds a B.S. in Hospitality Management from Pacific Shores University (1993) and an Executive Leadership Certificate from the Kamehameha Institute of Business (2012). His mother Dorothy ran a small bookshop in Kailua; his father Robert (deceased 2015) was a civil engineer for the county. Chris was a competitive swimmer through college before discovering outrigger canoe racing in his late 20s. He met Laura at a hotel industry conference in 2000 and married her in 2002.

He operates with quiet authority forged across decades of managing spaces where everything depends on invisible coordination. He reads rooms before anyone realizes they have been read: a misaligned picture frame, a staff member whose smile is strained, a guest whose body language says the upgrade mattered. He processes stress internally, converting it into action rather than expression. The people closest to him know the most controlled version of Chris is often the most burdened one.

Grief reshaped him in ways he is still discovering. He did not fall apart when he lost Laura. He could not afford to, professionally or personally, and the fact bothers him sometimes more than the grief itself. He wears his wedding band on a chain around his neck under his clothes. He sees Dr. Mark Sullivan biweekly because he treats grief work the way he treats hotel maintenance: like getting the car serviced.

He holds himself to standards that would be unreasonable applied to anyone else, which is the point. He believes the person setting the standard should always exceed it. He believes leadership is fundamentally an act of service, not authority. He believes how a person treats people who can do nothing for them is the only reliable measure of character.

## Work & Projects

Chris is the General Manager of the Palmside Honolulu Resort & Spa, a 320-room luxury property on Waikiki Beach within the Coral Crest Hospitality Group. He has held the role for six years. The property includes three restaurants, a full-service spa, a pool complex, event spaces, and a beach activities concession.

He oversees more than 280 employees across front desk, housekeeping, food and beverage, spa, maintenance, sales and marketing, and finance, working through Derek Sato as Director of Operations. Post-pandemic tourism recovery is strong, though labor shortages persist. A $4.2M renovation of the lobby and main restaurant is planned for Q1 2027. Guest satisfaction scores sit consistently in the top 15% of Coral Crest properties.

He was previously Director of Operations at the Leilani Grand in Maui (2007 to 2019) before Peggy Thornton recruited him to Palmside. His industry reputation centers on turning around underperforming properties. He is demanding but fair: he knows housekeepers' names and remembers their kids' milestones.

## Interests & Hobbies

Outrigger canoe paddling is Chris's primary passion outside work and closer to a spiritual practice than a sport. He paddles with Koa Kai Outrigger Canoe Club in the OC6 Masters Men's division, training five to six days a week between morning water sessions and dryland work. His crew of six has medaled at the Kaiwi Channel Relay, the Na Wahine O Ke Kai, and numerous local regattas. He has completed the Waikiki Roughwater Swim three times.

Woodworking occupies a different register. The workshop is where his hands do what his mind cannot, creating something tangible with grain and weight and imperfection from raw material. The hand tools passed down through his family carry a significance he does not articulate often.

Wine appreciation began as a professional necessity and became a genuine pleasure. He prefers Burgundy and Oregon Pinot Noir and belongs to a small group called the Oenophile Circle that meets the 2nd Saturday of each month.

Cooking at home is an act of inheritance: American comfort with Pacific influences, his grandmother's pot roast recipe is legendary among friends, Sunday morning pancake ritual maintained for twenty years. He hosts small dinner parties of six to eight people.

Reading runs to biographies of sports figures and leaders, historical fiction, and the occasional thriller. Currently reading "The Lighthouse Keeper" by D.M. Walsh, a hotelier biography. Quarterly book club with Mike and Brian, usually via video call.

## Preferences

- **Reading**: biographies, historical fiction, hospitality industry publications including "Coral & Key Magazine" and "Pacific Hospitality Review". Quarterly book club with Mike and Brian.
- **Shopping**: quality over quantity. Prefers local boutiques and local hardware stores. "Fashion fades, style does not."
- **Travel**: deep affection for Ireland, has been four times. Wants to take Hana to Dublin when she is old enough. Annual mainland trip to visit Ryan. Dreams of paddling in Tahiti.
- **Gifts**: thoughtful and specific. A book the recipient will love, a bottle from a winery they visited together, a handmade wooden piece from his workshop.
- **Food and drink**: balanced whole-foods diet, fish, rice, vegetables, fruit. Hearty American dishes at home including chowder, grilled fish, winter stew. Moderate wine drinker, glass of Pinot Noir most evenings. Sunday pancake ritual. Weakness for shave ice from Ono Icy in Kailua. Avoids heavy fried food and excessive sugar.
- **Aesthetic**: clean, classic, intentionally understated. Quality materials in neutral tones. Modern lines softened by personal artifacts. Strong opinions about lighting, sightlines, and the relationship between furniture and foot traffic.
- **Comfort and decompression**: the ocean is the primary reset. The lanai at sunset with nothing in his hands and nowhere to be. The workshop when the weight accumulates. Cooking a familiar recipe from memory without measuring, music playing in the background.
- **Sensory loves**: salt air, the particular golden quality of late-afternoon Honolulu light, the sound of water in any form, the smell of sandalwood, the grain of well-aged koa wood, the warmth of a ceramic mug against both palms at dawn. Dislikes: unnecessary noise, harsh artificial lighting, technology that creates problems while solving others.
- **Social style**: small, deep circle. Listens more than he speaks in groups. Remembers details about people they have forgotten sharing.
- **Pet peeves**: lateness (unforgivable), sloppy email writing, people who say "I'll try" instead of committing yes or no, anyone who treats hotel service staff disrespectfully.
